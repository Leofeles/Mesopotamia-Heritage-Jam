Some History

Mesopotamia was a civilization that stood in the mid east for millennia. Its rise and fall took thousands of years and over that time human civilization transformed. Mesopotamia is often referred to as the cradle of civilization. Marking a time in human history when written language begun to emerge, complex systems of trade and agriculture were refined. Technological advancements allowed for cities to grow and specialize.

Mesopotamia has the first documented religion. This civilization had a polytheistic approach to theistic practice, with thousands of gods in its pantheon. Many gods as well have multiple names because of the two linguistic groups who dwelled in ancient Mesopotamia in the third millennium BC: the Sumerian speakers and the Akkadian speakers.

Mesopotamian conceptions of the gods in relation to humans took place on three levels of existence. First the Mythic level second the cosmic level then local level each of these levels explains a facet of human and god interaction. The humanâ€™s responsibility to the gods was to feed them. These offerings were brought to large temples referred to as Ziggurats.

Ziggurats had many social functions as well as theistic. These temples often were the center of the city, because of the offerings that were left there for the gods. The greater social fabric of Mesopotamian society also revolved around these temples with rations of food being distributed from the citizens.

Let's Take a Look at Some Gods and Places of Theistic Practice

Utu/Shamash

The god Utu/Shamash was regionally connected to the city of Sippar, cosmically associated with the sun, and in charge of the trait of justice.

Enki/Ea

The god Enki/Ea was connected to the city of Eridu, the sweet waters, and wisdom.

Inanna/Ishtar

The goddess Inanna/Ishtar was associated with the city of Uruk, the planet Venus, and love and war.

Ziggurats

The places that people let offerings for the gods. In food and also were they got their daily rations.
